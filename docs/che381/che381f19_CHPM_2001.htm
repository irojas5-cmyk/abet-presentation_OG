1.0 INTRODUCTION
1.1 HISTORY OF THE OSHA LABORATORY STANDARD
On November 25, 1983, the Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) published the Hazard Communication Standard that applied to certain manufacturers and in part to certain laboratories. OSHA received several comments as to whether the procedures of the standard should apply to laboratories where the staff is usually highly educated. OSHA decided although "...31.9% of all laboratory workers have bachelors degrees, 20.6% have masters degrees, and 20.9% have doctorates..." that, "...there is some question as to whether laboratory workers actually make themselves as knowledgeable as they should be and some laboratory employees are not professionally trained."
(51 FR 26664).
Other unique differences for laboratories were noted including: the small amounts of chemicals used; the vast numbers of different chemicals involved; and that nearly half of the laboratories in one (OSHA) survey could not accurately predict their chemical needs even one month in advance.
On January 31, 1990, The Department of Labor published in the Federal Register an amendment to 29 CFR 1910, Subpart Z, identified as Section 1910.1450. The title of that amendment is
"Occupational exposure to hazardous chemicals in the laboratory," but is better known as the "Laboratory Standard." (Attachment A). A part of that standard is the requirement of a chemical hygiene plan that must be developed and implemented by January 31, 1991.
1.2 SCOPE AND APPLICATION
    • Applies to all employers whose laboratories use hazardous chemicals.
    • Amends (for laboratories) all other provisions of 29 CFR 1910 Subpart Z except:
      • For any OSHA health standard, only the requirement to limit employee exposure to the specific permissible exposure limit (PEL) shall apply for laboratories.
      • Prohibition of eye and skin contact where specified by any OSHA health standard shall be observed.
    • Does not apply to activities that do not fit the term "laboratory use."
    • Exempts laboratories which have and implement a Chemical Hygiene Plan from other regulations under 29 CFR 1910 Subpart Z, except for any activities which do not match OSHA's definition of "laboratory use of hazardous chemicals" (even though the activity is done inside a laboratory).
    • Where the action level or the PEL is routinely exceeded for an OSHA regulated substance, the monitoring and medical surveillance parts of the Chemical Hygiene Plan will be applied.
 
1.3 OSHA REQUIREMENTS OF THE CHEMICAL HYGIENE PLAN
    • Where hazardous chemicals are used in the work place, the employer shall develop and carry out the provisions of a chemical hygiene plan that is:
      • Capable of protecting employees from health hazards associated with hazardous chemicals in that laboratory, and
      • Capable of keeping the exposures below the action level or PEL.
    • The Chemical Hygiene Plan must be readily accessible to employees.
    • The Chemical Hygiene Plan shall include each of the following elements and shall indicate specific measures the employer will take to ensure laboratory employee protection:
      • Standard operating procedures relevant to safety and health;
      • Criteria employer will use to implement control measures to reduce employee exposure to hazardous chemicals;
      • A requirement that fume hoods and other protective equipment are functioning properly and methods to be taken to ensure proper and adequate performance;
      • Provisions for employee training and information
      • Circumstances requiring prior approval from the employer before implementation;
      • Provisions for medical consultation and examination;
      • Designation of personnel responsible for implementation of the CHP;
      • Provisions for additional protection for employees working with particularly hazardous substances including:
        • select carcinogens
        • reproductive toxins
        • substances with high degree of acute toxicity
    • Specific consideration shall be given to the following provisions where appropriate:
      • Establishment of a designated area;
      • Use of containment devices such as fume hoods or glove boxes;
      • Procedures for safe removal of contaminated waste;
      • Decontamination procedures.
    • The employer shall review and update the plan on a yearly basis.
1.4 ROLE OF THE AAL CHEMICAL HYGIENE PLAN
This Chemical Hygiene Plan describes American Air Liquide's safety program, including (but not limited to) chemical safety rules, personal protective equipment, control equipment (i.e., fume hoods), employee training programs, and medical programs. The Chemical Hygiene Plan is designed as a tool to coordinate safety procedures. Every employee in the laboratory must be trained in the applicable details of this Plan and must comply with its provisions.

4.0 SAFETY PROCEDURES AND GUIDELINES
4.1 EMERGENCY PROCEDURES: SPILLS & EXPOSURES
A. CHEMICAL SPILL OR RELEASE
Spills of toxic substances or accidents involving any hazardous chemical should be resolved immediately. The nature of the accident/spill will dictate the appropriate response. In the case of a sizable spill/release of a flammable chemical/gas (e.g., 4 liters of Acetone), or toxic chemical/gas (e.g., 4 liters of Chloroform) the following brief steps will be followed:
    • Evacuate the immediate area.
    • Dial "0" (Facility Operator), advise nature of emergency, and location of emergency (e.g., "Chemical Spill, XYZ lab").
    • The Operator will notify all employees (on the Public Address system) to evacuate, and will activate the Emergency Response Team.
    • Immediately exit the lab and deactivate the lab power by pushing the emergency (red) power button outside of the lab.
    • Evacuate the building through the nearest and safest exit.
    • Unless otherwise notified (on the P.A. system), assemble in front of the facility at the middle driveway.
    • Report to the department secretary:
(Carolyn Lewandowski, Wanda Kucharczyk, alternate)
This is basically the same procedure as the Facility Emergency Information in Attachment C.
B. CHEMICAL EXPOSURE
In the case of a chemical exposure (of a worker), the following steps should be observed:
    • Dial "0" (Facility Operator), advise nature of emergency, and location of emergency
(E.g., "Person with severe chemical burns, ABC lab").
    • Operator calls Fire Department Paramedics and notifies Emergency Response Team.
    • If there are no apparent dangers to yourself of chemical exposure, the following may be performed until the E.R.T./Paramedics arrive:
      • If a person has been splashed with a chemical, wash them with plenty of water for at least 15 minutes, removing all contaminated clothing (Eyewashes for eye/face splashes, Emergency Shower for all other).
      • If a person has been overexposed by inhalation, get victim to fresh air (if it is safe for you to enter area), apply artificial respiration if necessary.
B. CHEMICAL EXPOSURE (continued)
There are some fundamental actions that must NOT be used in handling emergencies.
    • DO NOT force any liquids into the mouth of an unconscious person.
    • DO NOT handle emergencies alone, especially without notifying someone that an accident has occurred.
    • DO NOT apply medical aid procedures without some training in that area (except to wash with WATER for 15 minutes and get victim to fresh air). If you are not trained in basic first aid, get MEDICAL direction before inducing vomiting, giving antidotes, or applying a "neutralizer" to the skin or eyes of the victim. Everyone should be trained in CPR.
    • DO NOT linger at the accident scene if you are not one of the emergency responders.
 
4.2 GENERAL SAFETY RULES & PROCEDURES
A. GENERAL SAFETY RULES
    • The Applied Technology Center is a designated "NO-SMOKING" facility. No smoking is allowed in any of the buildings. Smoking in designated areas only.
    • There will be no eating or drinking allowed in areas where chemicals are either stored or used. Because chemical vapors can be absorbed by foodstuff (especially bread), no food products should be allowed into chemical areas. Instead of designating laboratory and stock areas as "No Food", ALL areas should be considered as "No Food or Beverage" except those few areas actually designated as "Food and Beverage Permitted".
    • All safety rules and guidelines as found in the Air Liquide America Corporation (ALAC) "Employee Safety Guidelines" handbook apply in ADDITION to those listed in the chemical hygiene plan. The ALAC handbook is issued to all employees on their first day of employment.
    • Safety Glasses and Safety Shoes are required in all laboratories and storage areas. (See pages 16-17 for policy)
    • Common laboratory practice is to have someone working with you in the laboratory. If that is not possible, then someone should know of the solitary worker and check on him/her at regular intervals. Never work alone in a laboratory after hours or on weekends
    • Working after-hours or on the weekend in the office is not encouraged, however it is recognized that circumstances may warrant it periodically. Temporary and student workers are allowed to work in the office areas between 8AM and 6PM on weekdays, but are not allowed to be in the facility on weekends. Exceptions can be made if agreed upon by the supervisor and with Ron Nowak's approval (security card change)
    • Observe facility policy when entering someone else's lab or admitting non-employees to any lab. This policy can be found in Attachment D.
    • In the event that a facility evacuation is necessary, the procedure detailed in Attachment C ("Facility Emergency Information") must be followed.
B. GENERAL CHEMICAL SAFETY RULES
AWARENESS IS THE MOST FUNDAMENTAL RULE OF CHEMICAL SAFETY.
Everyone should remain constantly aware of:
    • A chemical's hazards, as determined from the MSDS and other appropriate references.
    • Appropriate safeguards for using that chemical, including personal protective equipment (PPE).
    • Location and proper use of emergency equipment (e.g., Eyewashes, Body Showers, Fire Extinguishers, Spill Kits).
    • How and where to properly store the chemical when not in use. (Chemicals are often used for only minutes in the average workday, the rest of the time it is being "stored".)
    • Proper personal hygiene practices.
    • The proper methods of transporting chemicals (and cylinders) within the facility.
    • Appropriate emergency procedures, including evacuation routes, spill cleanup procedures, and proper waste disposal methods.
Common practice is to have someone working with you in the laboratory. If that is not possible, then someone should know of the solitary worker and check on him/her at regular intervals (see p. 26).
NEVER work alone in a laboratory after hours or on weekends.
C. PERSONAL HYGIENE
Personal hygiene is an important factor in chemical hygiene. There are four routes of entry (inhalation, ingestion, injection, and eye or skin contact). If we properly protect ourselves, we can eliminate the chemical's ability to do harm. Personal hygiene practices include:
    • Wash promptly if skin contact is made with any chemical, regardless of corrosivity.
    • Wear appropriate eye protection at all times.
    • Avoid inhalation of chemicals, do not "sniff" test chemicals.
    • Do not mouth pipette anything, use suction bulbs.
    • Wash well before leaving the laboratory; do not wash with solvents, use soap.
    • Change clothing as soon as possible after leaving laboratory, and launder work clothes often.
    • Do not eat or drink in chemical areas.
    • Do not bring food, beverage or tobacco products into chemical storage or use areas.
D. PERSONAL PROTECTIVE EQUIPMENT (PPE)
The lab supervisor specifies most personal protective equipment (PPE) when needed and where necessary. It is the responsibility of each employee to be certain that the appropriate equipment is worn.
The most fundamental piece of personal protective gear is the normal clothing worn in the laboratory. Clothing should minimize exposed skin surfaces available for direct contact through splashing. Therefore, all employees should wear long sleeve/long legged clothing and avoid short sleeved shirts, short trousers or skirts when working with chemicals.
SAFETY GLASSES AND SAFETY SHOES MUST BE WORN AT ALL TIMES IN THE LABORATORIES.
SAFETY GLASSES POLICY:
    1. Eye protection must be worn at all times except for the following:
      2. If you are working at a computer that is not in the vicinity of an ongoing experiment. This exception is no longer valid when the person get up or moves around the lab. The distance that 'in the vicinity' translates to is left up to the judgement of the person and lab supervisor.
    2. Side shields, face masks, and other forms of extra protection:
      3. These items are to be worn at the discretion of the employee and lab supervisor. It is advised that each person wears the eye PPE suitable for the type of work being performed. If there is any question as to whether side shields are needed for a particular experiment, then it is always best to wear them.
    3. Visitors:
Visitors must follow the same standards as stated above for all Air Liquide employees. Please use good judgement in not exposing visitors to hazardous experiments or areas of the laboratory.
SAFETY SHOE POLICY
    1. Foot protection must be worn at all times except for the following:
      2. If any person has a medical condition that might prevent them from wearing safety shoes while in the lab. Due to the fact that many safety shoe designs are as comfortable and lightweight as normal shoes, there is not any valid exception other than health reasons to not practice safety first and wear safety shoes at all times.
    2. Visitors: Short-term visitors may be exempt from wearing foot protection. This is provided that they are accompanied while in the laboratory and that they are not taken near any potential hazards. The best policy in general is to not be performing any hazardous experiments or moving heavy equipment while hosting visitors.
Additional PPE available from American Air Liquide include:
    • Lab Coats- Long-sleeved lab coats offer the wearer minimal skin protection against minor splashes, but allow the chemical to react with the lab coat before reaching the skin, offering the victim time to remove the coat and use the Safety Shower.
Additional PPE which should be ordered when appropriate by the lab supervisor or lab worker:
    • Gloves- must be of a material compatible with the chemicals used. Rubber or plastic gloves should be inflated (by whipping in air, not by mouth inflation) to check integrity before each use. For high heat uses, gloves of the leather welding type or aluminized-type (Zetex) should be used. Heavily insulated gloves (Cryo-gloves) should be used for low temperature or cryogenic atmosphere work.
    • Splash Aprons- rubberized aprons offer additional time to react to a splash than a lab coat alone. Aprons should be worn in addition to the lab coat.
    • Face Shields- for more hazardous chemicals, corrosives, hot chemicals, and cryogenics, both safety glasses and face shields must be worn.
    • Ear Protection- if excessive noise is present, appropriate ear protection must be worn.
RULES ON RESPIRATORS
Respirators are generally not to be used in laboratories for normal work procedures. Any chemicals used in the lab which could be expected to be present in the lab atmosphere at levels which meet or exceed the PEL must be used in a fume hood, or the immediate area be provided with an exhaust drop, or similar appropriate device (i.e., "Engineered Control").
Spills/releases that necessitate respiratory protection should be handled by the E.R.T. (Emergency Response Team). Respirators shall be selected and used in accordance with the requirements of 29 CFR 1910.134. (Revised in 1998). Emergency Response Team members should check their respirators monthly to make sure it is in proper condition.
E. LABORATORY HOUSEKEEPING
Common housekeeping practices contribute greatly towards chemical hygiene and safety. A clean work area is much safer than a cluttered or dirty one. Some appropriate measures include:
    • Keep all aisles, hallways, and stairs clear of all chemicals.
    • Keep all work areas and especially workbenches clear of clutter and obstructions.
    • All working surfaces and floors should be cleaned regularly.
    • Access to emergency equipment, showers, eyewashes and exits should never be blocked by anything.
    • Wastes should be properly labeled, and kept in the proper containers.
    • Any unlabeled containers are considered wastes by the end of each workday.
Laboratory staff should be considerate of the janitorial staff. For the safety of the housekeeping staff, laboratory workers should make sure that:
    • All chemicals are placed in proper storage areas by the end of each workday.
    • All chemical containers are labeled with both the identity of the chemical and its hazards.
    • All spills are promptly cleaned up and the spilled chemical is properly disposed of.
    • No chemicals are to be stored in aisles, stairwells, on desks or workbenches, on floors or in hallways, or left on shelves over workbenches.
    • If a chemical or experiment cannot be removed or otherwise secured, and poses a reasonable threat to the safety of the janitorial staff, the following should be observed:
      • Place any wastebaskets that require emptying immediately outside of the lab, or empty them yourself.
      • Place a sign on the door warning not to enter (e.g., "Janitor - Danger Do Not Enter").
      • Lock the lab door(s) at the end of the workday.
F. HAZARD EVALUATION
With respect to labels and Material Safety Data Sheets:
    • Labels on incoming hazardous chemicals are not to be removed or defaced.
    • American Air Liquide will maintain any MSDS's received and make them readily available to employees (see section 3.1).
The following provisions shall apply to chemical substances developed in the laboratory:
    • If the composition of a chemical substance produced for laboratory use is known and determined to be hazardous, AAL shall provide appropriate training.

H. WORK PERMITS/PRIOR APPROVAL
The following guidelines summarize the requirements for prior approval and work permits for laboratory experiments and laboratory procedures. All work permits will have a one-year lifetime, after which they are reviewed/updated and renewed for another year or canceled. Work cannot be performed under an expired permit. Responsibility for renewal rests with the group manager.
    • PRIOR APPROVAL: Approval to proceed with a laboratory task should be obtained when:
      • There is a new procedure, process or test, even if it is very similar to older practices;
      • There is a change or substitution of any of the ingredient chemicals in a procedure;
      • There is a substantial change in the amount of chemicals used; usually one should review safety practices if the volume of chemicals used increases by 20 or 25%;
      • There is a failure of any of the equipment used in the process, especially safeguards such as fume hoods or clamp apparatus.
      • There are unexpected ("unusual") test results. When a test result is different than the predicted, a review of how the new result impacts safety practices must be made;
      • The members of the laboratory staff become ill, suspect exposure, smell chemicals, or otherwise suspect a failure of engineered safeguards.
Any new procedure should be subjected to review to assure that all safety considerations are in place
prior to implementation. The Facility Manager upon review of the pertinent work permit shall grant approval.
    • WORK PERMIT
      • Work permits are required for new research applications and testing of new equipment for which a potential hazard exists. Work permits are also required for movement of hazardous projects or tests to a new area of the facility.
      • Employees involved in the project must read the permit and be aware of hazards involved and also be properly trained in the procedure. They must sign that they have read and understood the work permit.
      • Hazardous chemicals used in the procedure require obtaining and filing the appropriate "Material Safety Data Sheet" (MSDS). This document must be recorded on the permit.
      • Repetitious projects performed in the same lab will not require new permits unless the scope of the project changes.
      • The Project Manager and Department Head shall approve all work permits, and will be held responsible if a work permit is not used.
      • The work permit shall provide any pertinent schematics, drawings, etc.

J. SAFE CHEMICAL HANDLING AND LABELING
Generally, safe chemical handling is outlined in "Prudent Practices for Handling Hazardous Chemicals in Laboratories" (see Section 9.0). Also pertinent is the Air Liquide America Corp. "Employee Safety Guidelines" handbook.
Correct Labeling of Chemicals
    • All chemical containers (everywhere in the facility) must be labeled at all times with the correct chemical name and constitution if a mix (e.g., concentrations), date prepared or stored, and name of group. This information must be written on the correct color-coded tape for groups if the item is placed at any time anywhere except within that group's laboratory.
    • No unlabeled chemical container should exist at anytime even for a moment!
    • Beakers, reactors, vials, or other containers being used to conduct experiments or just temporarily hold chemicals must be marked to identify chemical contents (e.g., if you are working with a beaker that contains Sulfuric acid, simply mark the beaker "Sulfuric acid" or "H2SO4". This will help prevent injuries or improper disposal). If the container is too small for adequate markings (e.g., a 10-ml sample vial), then mark it with a code number and record the contents in a lab notebook.

    • All personnel handling any chemical must read the MSDS sheet for that chemical and sign that they have done so (covered also by the Work Permit form), must have read the Chemical Hygiene Plan, must have read the Attachments and Appendices within the CHP and have discussed these with their group leader, must be trained in the proper handling of this chemical by their group leader, have the approval for use from their group leader, and thus be expected to be fully aware of all hazards of handling the chemical as well as the correct method of disposal. 4.3 LABORATORY SAFEGUARDS AND CHEMICAL CONTROL
Chemical safety is accomplished by awareness of the hazards, and by keeping the chemicals under control through a variety of engineered safeguards. Laboratory workers should be familiar with the proper use of those safeguards. Laboratory supervisors must be able to detect the malfunction of those safeguards. All engineered safeguards must be properly maintained, inspected on a regular basis, and never overloaded beyond their design limits.
A. VENTILATION/FUME HOODS
    • General laboratory room ventilation should normally be not less than 20 linear feet per minute airflow, thus providing comfortable breathing air for workers. However, the flow is not sufficient to prevent accumulation of chemical vapors. Therefore, work with chemicals having low threshold limit values (TLVs) or high vapor pressures must be done in a fume hood. Spot ventilation (commonly referred to as snorkels or elephant trunks) may also be used with caution, but consideration for the nature and volume of chemicals present should be taken into account.
    • To insure the safety of each, employer and employee both must be aware of the following guidelines regarding fume hoods:
      • Fume hoods should provide 80-120 linear feet per minute airflow.
      • The fume hood is a safety backup device to the condensers, traps, or other devices designed to trap and collect flammable or toxic vapors.
      • Fume hood shields should be lowered at all times except when adjusting the apparatus inside.
      • Experimental apparatus inside the hood should be kept towards the rear of the hood to prevent vapors from escaping.
      • The design of the hood is usually for substances of specific characteristics (e.g., venting at the top or bottom sides is for chemicals of low and high vapor density, respectively).
      • Hoods are NOT STORAGE AREAS (except in the case of the Carcinogens hood).
      • Vent ducts and fans must be kept clean and clear of obstructions.
      • The hood must remain "on" at all times when a chemical is inside the hood, regardless whether any work is being done in the hood.
    • Personnel should be aware of steps to take in the event of an emergency (e.g., power failure or other hood failures).
    • Every hood must be equipped with a vaneometer (an instrument for measuring the airflow) to ensure the hood is functioning properly. Laboratory personnel must also keep a monthly record of the hood performance.
    • In addition to hood performance checks carried out by lab personnel, an independent inspection will be conducted every 3 months on all fume hoods and snorkels in the facility. A selected team of company personnel will conduct the inspection. Air flow measurements on fume hoods and snorkels are obtained using an anemometer and recorded. The inspector then compares his or her measurement with the monthly vaneometer readings. The readings should correspond to one another, ensuring the laboratory vaneometer is functioning properly. Snorkels that are small and not equipped with vaneometers are simply checked for proper flow rate by the inspector, and recorded.
    • Following the above inspection procedure every 3 months, an evaluation of fume hood and snorkel performance is made. If a malfunction (noticeable difference between recorded flow rates or an indication that the flow rate is steadily decreasing with time) is observed, the laboratory supervisor and all users are immediately notified. In the case of a malfunctioning hood/snorkel, a shutdown procedure/tag-out should be followed (refer to laboratory work permit), and work must be avoided until proper repairs are made.
B. EYEWASHES AND SAFETY SHOWERS
Wherever chemicals have the possibility of damaging the skin or eyes, an emergency supply of water must be available. All laboratories that handle chemicals must be equipped with eyewashes and safety showers. As with any safety equipment, these can only be useful if they are accessible, therefore:
    • Keep all passageways to the eyewash and shower clear of any obstacle (even a temporarily parked cylinder cart).
    • Eyewashes should be checked routinely to be certain that water flows through.
    • Showers must be checked routinely (Once a month) to assure that access is not restricted and the start handle is within reach.
    • The flow through the safety showers should be tested regularly to ensure a flow of 30 gallons per minute for fixed showers, 3 gallons per minute for hand-held washes.
C. VAPOR DETECTION
Because odor thresholds can be greater than the TLV's (Threshold Limit Value), odors are not to be used as the primary methods of vapor detection. If suspicious odors are noticed, the investigators should obtain mechanical vapor detectors, such as detector tubes ("Draeger tubes") or detection meters. Location and nature of the suspect vapor will dictate respiratory protection.

D. EMERGENCY WARNING & EMERGENCY SHUTOFF BUTTONS
Common laboratory practice is to have at least two people working in a laboratory, for safety reasons. However, due to the number of staff at CRC, this often is not possible. We have addressed this issue in two ways: (1) when working alone on a potentially hazardous experiment, please notify at least one other person of what you are doing and for how long. The person should periodically check up on the person in the lab to make sure everything is all right. (2) if there is an emergency and assistance is needed, (e.g., chemical burn), press the "Emergency Warning" button located near the safety showers in every lab. 4.4 CRITERIA FOR PPE USE AND ENGINEERING CONTROLS
This section examines criteria and guidelines that can be used to determine the use of engineered controls and personal protective equipment.
A. EXPOSURE GUIDELINES
Most materials used have some guidelines for exposure, such as Threshold Limit Values (TLV) or Permissible Exposure Limits (PEL). When TLV or PEL values exist and are low, the user of a chemical must use it in an operating fume hood (or similar device such as a gas cabinet). When TLV or PEL
values are not available for that substance, the Lethal Dosage information (LD50) will be assessed. If that is low, then the chemical must also be used in a fume hood (or similar device). Whenever the chemical has a high vapor pressure, meaning that it evaporates quickly at room temperature, it will be used in a fume hood (or similar device). These controls are necessary even if the chemical with the high vapor pressure also has a very high TLV, PEL, or LD50 , because such chemicals are likely to reach their exposure limits in air at least as quickly as a chemical with low exposure guidelines and a low vapor pressure. Therefore, fume hoods will be used when:
    • The TLV or PEL is at or below 1000 ppm or 1000 mg/M3
    • The LD50 is below 250 mg/kg.
    • The vapor pressure is above 1 mm at 20 C.
Data for TLV, PEL, LD50 and vapor pressure can usually be found on the chemical's MSDS sheet. If none are listed on the MSDS, consult the reference materials listed in Section 9.0, or see A. Zdunek for assistance.
B. FIRE GUIDELINES
In general, a flammable chemical is determined by its flash point, the lowest temperature at which an ignition source can cause the chemical to ignite momentarily. Although the lowest temperature at which the chemical will catch fire with an ignition source is called the "fire point", it is rarely more than one or two degrees greater than the "flash point". Therefore, the flash point will be used as the reference of "fire hazard" here at American Air Liquide.
OSHA and NFPA have guidelines on when a chemical is considered flammable. Those guidelines are herein adopted for use in the laboratory.
    • "Flammable" is generally used to refer to chemicals with a flash point below 100° F. Chemicals with flash points between 100 and 200° F are termed "Combustible". Combustible chemicals have caused buildings to burn down; therefore, any chemical with a flash point below 200°F will be considered a "fire hazard" and will be stored in a flammable solvent storage area or flammable storage cabinet. They will be used in a vented fume hood, away from source of ignition.
C. REACTIVITY GUIDELINES
While NFPA has developed some guidelines on what constitutes a reactive chemical, their emphasis is centered on a fire emergency. Other guidelines on reactive chemicals can be found in regulations from the Department of Transportation (49 CFR) and the Environmental Protection Agency (40 CFR).
Here at American Air Liquide, a reactive chemical is one that is:
    • Ranked by NFPA a 3 or 4 for reactivity
    • Determined by the U.S. DOT as either:
      • An oxidizer
      • An organic peroxide
      • An explosive (Hazard Class 1 or Divisions 1.1 to 1.6)
      • Fits the U.S. EPA definition in 40 CFR 261
      • Fits the OSHA definitions of unstable or polymerizable
      • Is found to be reactive with ordinary substances
Once a chemical has been determined to be reactive, all proper safety precautions will be used including extra segregation in storage and prohibition on mixing with other chemicals without appropriate personal protection and precautions.
D. CORROSIVITY AND CONTACT HAZARDS
OSHA, DOT, and EPA specifically define a corrosive chemical. American Air Liquide will consider a chemical corrosive if it fits the definition of corrosive found in regulations by:
    • OSHA (29 CFR)
"Corrosive": A chemical that causes visible destruction of, or irreversible alterations in living tissue by chemical action at the site of contact. For example, a chemical is considered to be corrosive if, when tested on the intact skin of albino rabbits by the method described by the US DOT in Appendix A of 49 CFR part 173, it destroys or changes irreversibly the structure of the tissue at the site of contact following an exposure period of 4 hours. This term shall not refer to action on inanimate surfaces.
    • DOT (49 CFR)- See "Hazardous Materials Table"
    • EPA (40 CFR)
Waste: It is a liquid and corrodes steel (SAE 1020) at a rate greater than 6.35 mm (0.250 inch) per year (250 mpy) at a test temperature of 55°C (130°F).
Has a hazardous waste number of D002
    • or it has a very low or very high pH (Below 2.0 or above 12.5)
A skin or eye contact hazard chemical is one where the chemical's route for its toxic effects is through the skin or eyes. Chemicals, which are contact hazards, will be determined by examining medical and industrial hygiene literature (e.g., MSDS, and references listed in Section 8.0).

    5.1 SUSPECTED EXPOSURES TO TOXIC SUBSTANCES
    There are many times when employees suspect that they have been exposed to some toxic substance in the lab. Criteria to determine if a suspicion is "reasonable" have been developed, and are listed the immediately following section. If the circumstances surrounding the complaint are determined to cause a reasonable suspicion of exposure to a chemical, then the Chemical Hygiene Officer will initiate an Exposure Evaluation.
      1. CRITERIA OF "REASONABLE" SUSPICION OF EXPOSURE
    The following are examples of some events or circumstances that American Air Liquide will reasonably
    consider as evidence that an exposure to toxic substances is likely:
      • Victim had direct skin or eye contact with a chemical substance.
      • Odor was noticed, especially if person was working with any chemical that has a lower TLV than the odor threshold.
      • Manifestation of health hazard symptoms such as: headache, rash, nausea, coughing, tearing, irritation or redness of eyes, irritation of nose or throat, dizziness, loss of motor dexterity or judgment which resemble drunkenness, difficulty in breathing, etc.
      • Some or all symptoms disappear when person is taken away from the area and into fresh air.
      • Symptoms previously complained about reappear soon after person starts working with chemicals again.
      • Complaints are received from more than one person in the same work area.
      • It will be American Air Liquide policy to promptly investigate all complaints to determine risk of employee overexposure to the toxic substances in their workplace.
    5.2 EXPOSURE EVALUATIONS
    Once a complaint of possible hazardous chemical exposure has been received by the Facility Manager, the complaint will be documented in a short memo along with the decision of appropriate action. If it is decided that no further evaluation of the event is necessary, the reason for that decision will be included in the memo. If a decision is made by the Facility Manager and Chemical Hygiene Officer that the complaint should be investigated, then a formal Exposure Evaluation will commence.
      1. STEPS OF EXPOSURE EVALUATION
    A committee consisting of the Chemical Hygiene Officer and three persons from the Work Permit Approval committee will perform the Exposure Evaluation. The Exposure Evaluation Committee will then interview the person initiating the complaint and the victim (if it is not the same person). The Exposure Evaluation Form (Attachment K) will be used, and any other pertinent information will also be considered (e.g., if the exposure was due to some sort of accident, the supervisor's accident report will be reviewed).
    NOTE: The use or failure to use protective equipment, control measures and follow safety guidelines
    might contribute to the cause of overexposure. It is not the Exposure Evaluation Committee who places blame for the event. Blame, if appropriate, should come much later and from the
    R&D Safety Committee. THE PURPOSE OF THE EXPOSURE EVALUATION IS TO DETERMINE IF ANY EXPOSURE HAS TAKEN PLACE; NOT TO ASSIGN BLAME.
      1. NOTIFICATION
    The employees must be notified of the results of any air monitoring performed (in conjunction with an
    Exposure Evaluation) within 15 days of receipt of those results.

      • Information provided to the physician - The employer shall provide the following information to the physician:
        • The identity of the hazardous chemicals to which the employee may have been exposed;
        • A description of the conditions under which the exposure occurred;
        • A description of the signs and symptoms of exposure the employee is experiencing, if any.
      • Physician's written opinion including:
        • Any recommendation for further medical follow-up;
        • The results of the examination and any associated tests;
        • Any medical condition which may be revealed in the course of the examination which may place the employee at increased risk;
        • A statement that the employee has been informed by the physician of the results of the examination and any medical condition that may require further examination or treatment.
        • The written opinion shall not reveal specific findings of diagnoses unrelated to occupational exposure.
    The medical report will be sent directly to the Manager of Administration who will pass the appropriate information along to those involved.
      1. DOCUMENTATION
    All memos, notes and reports related to a complaint of possible exposure to toxic substances must be maintained in a file for easy retrieval with a cross-reference in the victim's personnel file.
      1. NOTIFICATION
    The employee shall be notified of the results of any medical examination with regard to any medical condition that might exist from overexposure to a chemical. AMERICAN AIR LIQUIDE will advise the employee of the investigation and possible corrective action to prevent future occurrences. Should the employee be negligent in following established safety rules or procedures, subsequent disciplinary procedures will follow.

    6.0 EMPLOYEE INFORMATION AND TRAINING
    As referenced in 29 CFR 1910.1450:
      • The employer shall provide employees with information and training to ensure that they are appraised of the hazards of chemicals in their work area.
      • The training and education program should be a regular, continuing activity, not simply an annual presentation.
      • Information - Employees shall be informed of:
        • The contents of this standard;
        • The location and availability of the Chemical Hygiene Plan;
        • The PEL's for OSHA regulated substances or recommended exposure limits for other hazardous chemicals where PEL's do not exist;
        • Signs and symptoms associated with exposures to the hazardous chemicals used in the laboratory;
        • The location and availability of known reference materials including MSDS sheets, but not limited to them (i.e., MSDS sheets).
      • Training - Employee training shall include:
        • Methods and observations that may be used to detect the presence or release of a hazardous chemical;
        • The physical and health hazards of chemicals in the work area;
        • Measures employees can use to protect themselves from these hazards, including specific procedures such as appropriate work practices, emergency procedures, and personal protective equipment to be used.
    6.1 EMPLOYEE INFORMATION AND SPECIFIC TRAINING PROCEDURES
    All American Air Liquide R&D employees working in laboratories who are potentially exposed to chemicals, including all R&D employees handling or using chemicals, will be trained in handling, storage, hazards of, and disposal of all chemicals in their work areas. This training will consist of:
      • Initial training when an employee is first hired.
      • Participation through group safety meetings held at least monthly in which the specific chemical hazards for each group are discussed and made clear to all employees, along with proper procedures and updates for safe handling, storage, and disposal.
        • The AAL Chemical Hygiene Plan shall be presented at one group meeting annually.
        • Personal safety and protection, full knowledge of all existing chemical hazards, and emergency procedures will be stressed in the group meetings.
      • Means of monitoring for exposure, and the correct response of both the employee and American Air Liquide will be explained the group meetings.
      • Additional training if needed for handling specific chemicals with a particular hazard such as high toxicity. This training can be coordinated with Alan Zdunek, the Chemical Hygiene Officer.

    7.0 RECORDS AND RECORDKEEPING
    7.1 RECORDS
    Specific records will be kept for at least as long as an employee is employed at this facility. These include:
    Repair and Maintenance Records for Control Systems - These demonstrate that equipment such as fume hoods are well maintained and kept in proper operating order. These records may be maintained by retaining copies of the service invoices.
    Complaints from Employees - Even if the complaint is found to be unjustified, it is desirable to have a record of the complaint, and any ensuing investigation or outcome. The complaints should be limited to chemical exposure, inoperative (or poorly designed) engineered controls, defective personal protection equipment, or equipment purchased from a vendor that poses a safety hazard due to design flaw or specific application (e.g., a cylinder holder which easily becomes un-secured). Employee complaints should be in writing, and copies sent to the Facility Manager, the Director of Research
    Accident Reports - The lab supervisor must report any accidents in writing within 24 hours of the occurrence. This includes chemical exposures as well as incidents involving physical hazards (e.g., cuts, falls, electrical shock, etc). Copies must be sent to the Director of Research, and the Facility Manager.
     
    7.2 RECORDKEEPING
    The Laboratory Standard requires that records be maintained of all Exposure Evaluations, Medical Consultations and reports, and that those records be maintained in accordance to 29 CFR 1910.1020. That section requires those records to be maintained for at least 30 years beyond the employee's time of employment and also describes the accessibility of the records. These records will be kept by the Facility Manager, and copies kept by the R&D Chemical Hygiene Officer and will be available for viewing by the involved parties or their authorized representatives (designated in writing). Employees will be permitted to examine only the records that pertain to them (i.e., if a person initiates a complaint of chemical exposure, but is not the victim, said person will not be allowed to examine the victim's medical records). If a person desires to examine records that do not directly pertain, they must secure a written release from the principal party ("victim").

    DEFINITIONS

    Below is a partial list of definitions as defined in the Laboratory Standard, 29 CFR 1910.1450 that are pertinent to American Air Liquide's activities. A complete list is given in Attachment A.
      • LABORATORY - Means any facility where the "laboratory use of hazardous chemicals" occurs. It is a workplace where relatively small quantities of hazardous chemicals are used on a non-production basis.
      • LABORATORY SCALE - Means work with substances in which the containers used for reactions, transfers, and other handling of substances are designed to be easily and safely manipulated by one person. "Laboratory scale" excludes those work places whose function is to produce commercial quantities of materials.
      • HAZARDOUS CHEMICAL means a chemical for which there is statistically significant evidence that an acute or chronic health effect may occur in exposed employees.
      • LABORATORY USE OF HAZARDOUS CHEMICALS - Means handling or use of such chemicals in which all of the following conditions are met:
      • Chemical manipulations are carried out on a laboratory scale,
      • Multiple chemical procedures or chemicals are used,
      • The procedures involved are not part of a production process, nor in any way simulate a production process,
      • Protective laboratory practices and equipment are available and in common use to minimize the potential for employee exposure to hazardous chemicals.
      • COMPRESSED GAS means:
        • A gas or gas mixture having, in a container, an absolute pressure >40 psi at 70°F
        • A gas or mixture of gases having, in a container, an absolute pressure exceeding 104 psi at 130°F regardless of the pressure at 70°F
        • A liquid having a vapor pressure >40 psi at 100°F as determined by ASTM D323-72.
      • FLAMMABLE GAS means:
        • A gas that, at ambient temperature and pressure, forms a flammable mixture with air at a concentration of 13% by volume or less.
        • A gas that, at ambient temperature and pressure, forms a range of flammable mixtures with air wider than 12% by volume, regardless of the lower limit.
      • FLAMMABLE LIQUID means any liquid having a flashpoint <100°F, except any mixture having components with flashpoints of >100°F, the total which make up 99% or more of the total volume.
      • COMBUSTIBLE LIQUID means any liquid having a flashpoint >100°F (37.8°C) but <200°F (93.3°C), except any mixture having components with flashpoints of >200°F, the total volume of which make up 99% or more of the total volume of the mixture.
      • FLASHPOINT means the minimum temperature at which a liquid gives off a vapor in sufficient concentration to ignite.
